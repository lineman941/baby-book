**Subject:** Whether Taylor County, Florida shows an abnormal cancer burden, and whether

## Bottom line up front

1. **The environmental history is real and heavily documented.** ✅ A pulp mill discharged on
   the order of 50 million gallons/day into the Fenholloway River for decades; the waste
   contained dioxins; a 1990 EPA study found river dioxin ~1,900× the standard; the river became
   a textbook endocrine‑disruption case (intersex fish). The 1947 Florida law that *legally
   designated the Fenholloway an industrial river* is real.
2. **The regulatory matter is current, not historical.** ✅ The mill stopped production in late
   2023, but its wastewater‑treatment system and groundwater monitoring remain active, and in
   **2026 FDEP issued a draft permit** that would let the owner discharge **up to 12 million
   gallons/day** back into the river at the original mill‑site location after it lost use of the
   coastal pipeline. This is a live dispute.
3. **The single most load‑bearing cancer number needs a like‑for‑like re‑pull.** 🟠 The
   "Taylor County 65.0 per 100,000, 2nd in Florida, 2018–2022" lung figure surfaced in the data as
   **late‑stage** incidence, while the Florida/national comparators (53.3) are **all‑stages**.
   A late‑stage‑vs‑all‑stages comparison is not valid and would overstate the excess. Pull the
   all‑stages table before this goes in any presentation.
4. **The water violation is real but does not point at the mill.** ✅/🟠 Perry's system had a
   *documented TTHM (trihalomethanes) violation* in Q1 2024. But TTHMs are **disinfection
   byproducts of chlorination**, not a pulp‑mill effluent — so this violation, on its own, is not
   evidence of mill contamination.
5. **The "young girls getting breast cancer" claim cannot currently be tested from public data.**
   ⛔ For a county Taylor's size, cancer rates for narrow age bands are typically **suppressed**
   (case counts of 1–9 are not published). Anecdote is not a cluster; a cluster needs case‑level
   data with a defined age range and denominator.
6. **No verified evidence connects the mill to the cancer burden or the water violation.** The
   exposure chain (source → chemical → release → transport → human exposure → dose → biological
   plausibility → observed cancer) is *not* demonstrated by anything found here.

## For litigation use — read this first

**This memo is a factual research organizer, not legal advice.** It is intended to help assemble
and triage evidence. Retain a licensed Florida environmental / toxic‑tort attorney and qualified
experts (epidemiologist, toxicologist, hydrogeologist) before relying on any of it. The
case‑dispositive records come through **public‑records requests and discovery**, not web search.

**Evidence tiers — know what you actually have.** For a lawsuit, the *source* of a fact matters as
much as the fact:

- **Tier 1 — Primary agency record (authenticable, potentially admissible):** FDEP permits/DMRs,
  EPA ECHO/decisions, the City of Perry CCR, State Cancer Profiles/FCDS, court dockets. **Obtain
  the actual document.** Everything below must be upgraded to Tier 1 before it goes in a filing.
- **Tier 2 — Secondary reporting (a lead, generally hearsay):** newspaper articles, NGO press
  releases, Wikipedia. Useful to find Tier‑1 documents; **not proof on its own.** Most citations
  in this memo are Tier 2 — treat them as a map to the primary record.
- **Tier 3 — Prior AI‑thread assertions (unverified until sourced):** exact figures/IDs that
  originated in the earlier ChatGPT conversation. Verify each against Tier 1.

**Learn from the case that already lost.** A **federal class action by 100+ residents over
contaminated well water was dismissed** because the homeowners **could not prove sufficient
financial damage** (Tier 2 — confirm the docket/citation). The two hardest elements here are the
same ones that sink environmental class actions generally: **(a) causation** (specific chemical →
specific pathway → specific plaintiffs' exposure → specific disease) and **(b) ascertainable,
provable damages / a certifiable class**. Build toward those from day one.

**Elements to map evidence to (general toxic‑tort framing — your attorney will refine):** duty /
standard of care; breach (permit violations, exceedances, concealment); **general causation** (can
the agent cause this disease — expert + literature); **specific causation** (did it cause *these*
plaintiffs' disease — exposure reconstruction + dose); damages; and the statutory hooks (e.g.
CWA/NPDES violations, Florida environmental statutes, negligence, nuisance, trespass, strict
liability for ultrahazardous activity). **Causation is currently the weakest link — it is not
established by anything in this memo.**

## Section 2 — Cancer data (Track A)

### 2.1 What is defensible now

- **⛔ "Highest lung‑cancer rate in the U.S." — not supported.** The prior thread itself
  retracted this. Union County, FL consistently ranks #1 in Florida for lung incidence, and even
  Union is not the national maximum. *(Union's rate is heavily influenced by its large
  incarcerated population — a known confounder for any small rural county comparison.)*
- **🟠 "Taylor County 65.0 per 100,000, rank 2, 2018–2022 (lung & bronchus)."** This figure
  surfaced in State Cancer Profiles tied to a **late‑stage** query context, not all‑stages. The
  comparators quoted alongside it (FL 53.3 / U.S. 53.3) are all‑stages numbers. **Do not present
  65.0 vs 53.3 as an apples‑to‑apples excess until the all‑stages Taylor figure is pulled.**
  Primary source: State Cancer Profiles, Lung & Bronchus, All Stages, 2018–2022, Florida by county —
  https://statecancerprofiles.cancer.gov/incidencerates/index.php?stateFIPS=12&areatype=county&cancer=047&stage=999&race=00&sex=0&age=001&year=0&type=incd&sortVariableName=rate&sortOrder=desc
- **🟡 Female lung & bronchus 49.0 per 100,000 (vs ~30 FL / ~30 U.S.).** Elevated female lung
  incidence is a plausible and notable signal, but this figure also surfaced with a late‑stage
  context in one search. Re‑pull sex‑specific, all‑stages before relying on it.
- **✅ Registry reporting lag is real.** Florida requires case reporting within ~6 months, but a
  *complete* diagnosis‑year file generally is not available until roughly **two years** after the
  year ends. **Do not fabricate a "2026 cancer rate."** Use the newest *complete* year and label
  its coverage.

### 2.1a Confounders you must adjust for (the prior thread ignored these)

- **✅ Small population → unstable, suppressed rates.** Taylor County's population is only
  **~21,796** (2020 Census). At that size, county rates for subgroups (age bands, sex‑specific,
  histology) are statistically unstable and are routinely **suppressed** when counts fall to 1–9.
  A single year's handful of cases can swing a rate dramatically. Confidence intervals and case
  counts must be shown, not just point rates.
- **🟡/⛔ Smoking is the dominant lung‑cancer driver — get Taylor's rate before attributing to the
  environment.** Rural North Florida generally has higher smoking prevalence than the state
  (Florida adult smoking was **~11.3% in 2022**). Lung cancer is overwhelmingly driven by tobacco;
  an elevated rural lung rate is *expected* on smoking grounds alone and must be adjusted for
  before any environmental attribution. Taylor County's specific adult‑smoking rate needs to be
  pulled (County Health Rankings / BRFSS). Until then, an environmental signal in lung cancer is
  **not separable** from a smoking signal.
- **✅ Union County comparison caveat.** Union County's #1 Florida lung rate is heavily inflated
  by its large incarcerated population — a reminder that rural small‑county rates need
  denominator/population scrutiny.

### 2.2 Breast cancer / "young cancer" (Track E)

- **⛔ Not establishable from public county rates at this time.** For a county of Taylor's
  population, breast‑cancer counts in narrow age bands (e.g. 20–29, 30–39) are almost certainly
  **suppressed** (1–9 cases not published) or statistically unstable. The claim "young girls are
  getting breast cancer" must be tested with **case‑level data and a defined age range**, not
  anecdote and not a suppressed rate. Treat this as an open question requiring a data request to
  the Florida Cancer Data System (FCDS), not as a finding.
- Age bands to request explicitly (do **not** merge children, teens, 20s, 30s, 40s into one
  "young" bucket): breast 20–29 / 30–39 / 40–49; lung in younger adults; pediatric/AYA cancers —
  each against the matching Florida and national baseline.

## Section 4 — The Fenholloway River & discharge history (Track C)

- **✅ 1947 "industrial river" law.** The Florida Legislature granted the right to "discharge and
  deposit sewage, industrial and chemical wastes and effluents … into the waters of the Fenholloway
  River, and the waters of the Gulf of Mexico into which said river flows." The river was
  *legally designated* to receive industrial waste to attract the mill.
- **✅ Discharge volume.** From 1954, the mill discharged **up to ~50 million gallons/day** of
  waste into the Fenholloway. *(EPA decision language cited in the prior thread describes ~50 MGD of
  treated effluent under the NPDES permit — consistent.)*
- **✅ Dioxin.** Waste contained **dioxins**, a byproduct of chlorine bleaching of pulp. A **1990
  EPA study found Fenholloway dioxin levels ~1,900× the agency's standard.**
- **✅ Class V designation.** The Fenholloway was designated a **Class V** water body (Navigation,
  Utility, and Industrial Use) — Florida's **least stringent** water‑quality class. The long
  restoration fight was about reclassifying it *back* toward Class III (recreation/fish/wildlife).
- **✅ Endocrine disruption.** The Fenholloway is a well‑known case of intersex fish (female fish
  developing male sex characteristics) — a documented biological effect, widely reported.
- **✅ Documented human‑exposure pathway: fish‑consumption advisory.** Government officials warned
  the public **not to eat Fenholloway fish** due to dioxin; for **largemouth bass the advisory was
  "DO NOT EAT."** EPA sent dioxin‑in‑fish data to Florida in **February 1989**; the state did not
  publicize it for ~18 months; in **September 1990** EPA urged Florida to warn people. 1989 EPA
  sampling reportedly found spots in the river where "nothing was alive." *(This is the single most
  concrete documented exposure route — anyone who consumed the fish. Authenticate the actual FDOH
  advisory and EPA study as Tier‑1.)*
- **✅ Private wells fouled.** Residents reported private drinking‑water wells running **"brown and
  smelly for years,"** which they blamed on the mill. *(Alleged, not court‑proven — see §12.)*
- **✅ NPDES permit.** The facility discharged under a federal **NPDES permit (base number
  FL0000876)**, first P&G, later Buckeye Florida, L.P. *(The exact current permit/application
  suffix cited in the prior thread — "FL0000876‑IW1S‑009‑NR" — is 🟡; confirm against FDEP/EPA.)*
- **✅ 1995 FDEP agreement → discharge relocation.** Under a **1995 agreement** with FDEP, the
  company agreed to process changes to reduce effluent coloration **and to relocate the wastewater
  discharge point** in the Fenholloway. *(This is the documented origin of the "the discharge was
  moved" observation.)*
- **✅ 2003 — company chose the pipeline over process cleanup.** In **December 2003**, Buckeye
  **rejected** government‑supported cleanup proposals (oxygen delignification to cut chlorine/dioxin,
  aerated‑sludge and possible wetlands treatment — backed by NRDC/Clean Water Network/FDEP/EPA) and
  **insisted on building a pipeline to discharge to the Gulf of Mexico** instead. *(Relevant to
  notice/intent and to the standard‑of‑care analysis.)*
- **✅ Coastal pipeline (Georgia‑Pacific, 2013→2020).** Georgia‑Pacific **purchased the mill in
  2013**, invested **over $300 million**, and completed the **"Fenholloway Water Quality Project"**
  — a **~15‑mile pipeline** routing wastewater to the **mouth of the Fenholloway**, bypassing most
  of the river — in **September 2020**. The river was subsequently reported as substantially
  cleaned up. *(This upgrades the earlier 🟡 dates to ✅.)*
- **🟡 Outfalls D‑001 / D‑002 and the "newer drain duct."** The specific outfall IDs and whether
  the discharge point observed being "moved farther away" is an **industrial‑wastewater outfall, a
  stormwater outfall, a treated‑effluent pipeline terminus, or a groundwater‑remediation
  discharge** must be read off the permit engineering drawings. **This distinction is essential and
  is currently unresolved.**

## Section 5 — Current 2026 status (the live matter)

- **✅ Wastewater system still operational.** Although the mill stopped manufacturing, the
  **primary/secondary/tertiary wastewater‑treatment system, non‑potable reuse, stormwater
  management, and groundwater monitoring remain active** and are the subject of a 2026 FDEP permit
  process.
- **✅ Pipeline access lost → move discharge back upstream.** After a legal/easement dispute,
  Georgia‑Pacific was **prohibited from using the coastal pipeline** and applied to **resume
  discharge at the original mill‑site location** — i.e., back into the river rather than at the
  Gulf‑ward pipeline terminus.
- **✅ Draft permit: up to 12 MGD.** In **January 2026**, FDEP released a **draft permit** that
  would allow discharge of **up to 12 million gallons/day** of treated wastewater/stormwater into
  the Fenholloway. FDEP later **responded to public comments** with changes including **more
  frequent discharge monitoring and additional/expanded groundwater‑monitoring wells**, and held a
  **public meeting**.
- **Open investigative question (documented, not speculative):** *Why does a mill that no longer
  manufactures still require an operating wastewater‑treatment system and groundwater monitoring?*
  The answer (residual industrial wastewater, contaminated groundwater, stormwater, remediation,
  or other) must come from the permit application and technical attachments — **do not assume it
  means active carcinogenic contamination.**

## Section 6 — Perry municipal drinking water (Track D)

- **✅ Source.** Perry's water comes from **wells in the Floridan Aquifer** (reported as three
  wells).
- **✅ Documented TTHM violation.** Perry's system **was in violation of the TTHM standard in Q1
  2024** (Jan 1–Mar 31) at the **700 Charles Hendry Road** monitoring location; EPA/EWG flags a
  "serious violation" for the period. Long‑term exposure to TTHMs/HAA5 above the MCL is associated
  with increased cancer risk (this is standard CCR language and is accurate).
- **🟡 Exact figures.** The specific values from the prior thread — **TTHM 83.01 ppb (MCL 80),
  HAA5 49.64 ppb (MCL 60), manganese 0.35 ppm (against 0.05, violation Nov 2023), arsenic 0.63
  ppb, chromium 0.59 ppb** — are **plausible and consistent with a real CCR, but were not directly
  confirmed here** (the CCR PDF and EWG page could not be fetched). Pull the report to finalize:
  City of Perry 2024 CCR — https://www.cityofperry.net/media/866 ; EWG (PWSID FL2620208) —
  https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/system.php?pws=FL2620208
- **🟠 Critical distinction — TTHMs do not implicate the mill.** TTHMs and HAA5 are
  **disinfection byproducts formed by chlorination of organic matter in the water**, not
  pulp‑mill effluent. A TTHM violation is a *water‑treatment* issue. It is **not** evidence of a
  mill‑to‑well pathway. Manganese is naturally common in Floridan Aquifer groundwater as well.
- **✅ Compliance ≠ proof of no historical exposure.** Correct framing to keep: a report showing
  present‑day compliance does not prove the absence of past contamination; conversely, a detected
  chemical does not prove it came from the mill. **Source and pathway must be established**, not
  assumed. The mill→groundwater→municipal‑well pathway is **unproven** and is a specific research
  target (well locations vs. plume vs. monitoring data), not a finding.
- **Also request:** historical sampling (not just the latest annual report), source‑well raw
  monitoring, PFAS / UCMR results, VOCs, metals, and any historical groundwater connection between
  the mill site and municipal supply.

## Section 8 — Cluster analysis (to be done with real data)

Treat as a **statistical question**, never anecdote‑as‑proof:
- female lung under 50 / under 40; breast under 50 / under 40; younger‑adult lung; pediatric/AYA;
  cancer deaths by age — each vs. the correct baseline, with confidence intervals and case counts,
  acknowledging small‑county suppression and instability. A "cluster" claim requires a defined
  population, time window, expected count, and observed count — not a list of individuals.
- **🟡 No public record found of a formal cancer‑cluster investigation for Taylor County.** A
  search surfaced no ATSDR or Florida DOH cancer‑cluster study specific to Taylor County/Perry.
  Florida DOH has a **"Cancer Concerns" / cancer‑cluster investigation** process that residents can
  **formally request** — doing so creates an official record and can compel a state statistical
  review. (FDOH Cancer Concerns — https://www.floridahealth.gov/statistics-data/cancer-research/cancer-concerns/)
- **⛔ Reality check on power.** With **~21,796 residents**, Taylor County is small enough that even
  a real environmental effect may not reach statistical significance in county‑level rates, and
  many subgroup rates will be suppressed. This cuts both ways in litigation: absence of a
  "statistically significant cluster" is **not** proof of no harm, but you also **cannot** assert a
  cluster from anecdote. Expert epidemiological analysis (possibly case‑level, possibly
  sub‑county / exposure‑zone) is required.
