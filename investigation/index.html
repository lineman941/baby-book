Bottom line up front

What the evidence does and does not show

1
The environmental history is real and heavily documented: ~50 million gallons/day of dioxin-bearing pulp waste into the Fenholloway for decades; a 1990 EPA study found river dioxin ~1,900× the standard; the river is a textbook intersex-fish endocrine case; a 1947 Florida law legally made the Fenholloway a Class-V “industrial river”; and an official “DO NOT EAT” fish advisory documents a real exposure route.
2
The regulatory matter is current. The mill stopped production in late 2023, but its wastewater-treatment system and groundwater monitoring remain active, and in 2026 FDEP issued a draft permit that would let the owner discharge up to 12 million gallons/day back into the river at the original mill-site location after it lost the coastal pipeline.
3
The most load-bearing cancer number needs a like-for-like re-pull. “Taylor County 65.0 per 100,000, 2nd in Florida, 2018–2022” surfaced as LATE-STAGE incidence, while the Florida/national comparators (53.3) are ALL-STAGES. That mismatch overstates the excess — pull the all-stages table first. Also: rural lung cancer is heavily driven by smoking, which must be adjusted for.
4
The water violation is real but does not point at the mill. Perry had a documented Q1-2024 trihalomethanes (TTHM) violation — but TTHMs are disinfection byproducts of chlorination, not pulp-mill effluent. It is a water-treatment issue, not evidence of a mill-to-well pathway.
5
The “young girls getting breast cancer” claim cannot be tested from public data yet. With only ~21,796 residents, narrow-age-band counts are suppressed (1–9 cases unpublished). A cluster needs case-level data with a defined age range — not anecdote.
6
No verified evidence connects the mill to the cancer burden or the water violation. The exposure chain — source → chemical → release → transport → human exposure → dose → biological plausibility → observed cancer — is not demonstrated here. This is the gap expert work must close.
